Favicon YouTube·Peppercoat BBQ 14:37 Equipment & Safety (Steps 16-30) Read your grill/smoker's instruction manual. Inspect your grill/smoker for cleanliness and function. Clean grates with a stiff brush to remove old debris. Clear ash from the bottom of charcoal grills. Check gas grill lines for leaks. Ensure propane tanks have enough gas, or have a spare tank. Oil the grates with a thin layer of cooking oil before heating. Set up a dedicated area for raw meat prep and a separate one for cooked meat. Wash all surfaces and utensils thoroughly before starting. Wash hands with soap and water for 20 seconds before handling food. Have a spray bottle of water nearby for flare-ups. Ensure proper ventilation if using a grill indoors (e.g., in a garage). Designate a helper if it's a large party. Set up a cooling station for the finished meat. Have a fire extinguisher nearby, just in case. Meat Prep - Trimming & Seasoning (Steps 31-50) Remove meat from packaging. Pat the entire surface of the meat dry with paper towels. For ribs, slide a dull knife under the membrane on the bone side. Grasp the membrane with a paper towel and pull it off. For briskets/pork butts, trim the fat cap to about 1/4 inch thickness. Remove any hard, large pieces of fat. Remove any silver skin from the meat surface. Trim any overly thin edges that might burn during the cook. Apply a binder (mustard, olive oil, hot sauce) if desired. Mix your chosen dry rub ingredients in a bowl. Apply the dry rub generously to all sides of the meat. Use your hands to gently press the rub onto the meat. Ensure every crease and corner is covered with rub. Place the seasoned meat on a pan or baking sheet. Cover the meat with plastic wrap or place it in a large bag. Refrigerate the meat to dry brine for at least an hour, or up to 24-36 hours. If short on time, season and cook immediately (skip the long brine). Before cooking, let the meat sit at room temperature for 15-30 minutes for even cooking. Prepare an injection liquid if you plan to inject the meat. Inject the meat in several places using a meat injector (optional). Phase II: The Cook (Steps 51-150) Fire Management & Setup (Steps 51-70) Start your fire early (plan for 1 hour for fire prep alone). Build a coal bed. Light your charcoal using a chimney starter or firelighters. Avoid using lighter fluid, which can impart off-flavors. Wait for the coals to be covered in a light gray ash before spreading. Set up your grill/smoker for indirect cooking (two-zone method). For the "snake method," line up coals in a semicircle around the edge of a kettle grill. Place a drip pan in the indirect heat zone. Add water, apple juice, or vinegar to the drip pan for moisture. Place a few wood chunks on the hot coals to start the smoke. Wait for the initial thick white smoke to dissipate. Aim for a thin, light blue, "sweet" smoke. Close the lid and preheat the smoker to the target temperature (usually 225°F-250°F). Use dampers/vents to control the air flow and temperature. Open vents to increase temperature. Close vents slightly to decrease temperature. Stabilize the temperature for 20-30 minutes before adding the meat. Place the meat on the grates, fat-side up or down depending on heat source. Insert a reliable, leave-in probe thermometer into the thickest part of the meat, avoiding bone. Close the lid and the cooking process begins. Monitoring & Cooking (Steps 71-130) Do not open the lid for at least the first hour or two. Trust your temperature probes, not the built-in grill thermometer (which can be inaccurate). Monitor the ambient temperature inside the smoker. Aim to maintain a consistent temperature between 225°F and 250°F. Add more charcoal/wood as needed to maintain heat and smoke. Check the fire every 45 minutes to an hour for long cooks. Spritz the meat with a 50/50 mix of apple cider vinegar and water every hour or so after the bark sets (around 2-3 hours in). The spritz helps keep the surface moist and builds a good bark. Notice the meat temperature "stall" (around 150°F-165°F internal temperature). The stall is normal and caused by evaporative cooling. Be patient during the stall; it can last hours. Do not increase the temperature drastically during the stall. If desired, prepare to wrap the meat when the bark looks good and dark (around 4-6 hours in, or 160°F IT). Lay out two large sheets of heavy-duty aluminum foil or butcher paper in a cross shape. Place the meat on the foil/paper. Spritz one last time before wrapping. Wrap the meat tightly, ensuring no tears in the foil. Double wrap if necessary to prevent moisture loss. Return the wrapped meat to the smoker. Reinsert the temperature probe. You can increase the temperature to 275°F to speed up the wrapped cook. Continue cooking until the target internal temperature is reached. The final temperature for most BBQ is between 195°F and 205°F. Check for tenderness using the temperature probe; it should slide in easily with little resistance. Do not rely solely on time; rely on temperature and tenderness. Time per pound is an estimate, not a rule. If making ribs, they are done when tender or bend easily with tongs. The bone should pull away easily from the meat when done. Prepare your BBQ sauce while the meat finishes cooking. If making sauce from scratch, simmer until reduced to the desired consistency. Remove the meat from the smoker once it hits the target temperature. Place the wrapped meat in a dry cooler or an empty pan. The Long Rest & Serving (Steps 131-200) Let the meat rest while still wrapped. Resting allows juices to redistribute throughout the meat. A large cut like a pork butt or brisket needs to rest for at least 30-60 minutes, potentially longer. The cooler acts as a "hot hold" to keep the temperature up. While resting, prepare your serving station and sides. Warm up your BBQ sauce in a small saucepan over low heat. Ensure sugar in the sauce is dissolved. Set out buns, coleslaw, and pickles. Once rested, carefully unwrap the meat, being mindful of hot steam and juice. Discard excess fat and the bone if it's a pork butt. If serving pulled pork, use meat claws or two forks to shred the meat. If serving brisket, slice it thinly against the grain. Separate the point and the flat of the brisket for different serving styles. Cube the point to make burnt ends (optional). Drizzle some of the reserved drippings or a thin vinegar sauce over the shredded/sliced meat. Mix to coat all the pieces evenly. Add more dry rub seasoning at this stage if needed (optional). Serve the meat on a bun with coleslaw and sauce on the side. For ribs, slice between the bones into individual ribs. Serve ribs by themselves or with extra sauce. Ensure all cooked meat is kept at a safe warm temperature if serving buffet style. Keep raw and cooked food cutting boards and utensils separate throughout the entire process.
